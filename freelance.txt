If his songs were genuinely hostile to Islam or Muslim identity, that would be a strange choice for a national anthem.

**The real answer to why Tagore didn't write "Islamic" songs:**

- He came from a Bengali Brahmo Samaj Hindu background, but his songs draw more from Baul mysticism, Vaishnava poetry, and Upanishadic universalism than from any organized religious doctrine — including orthodox Hinduism itself. He wasn't writing religious anthems for any single faith.
- His themes are nature, love, longing, patriotism, and a kind of personal spiritual humanism — not devotional content tied to any one religion's specific figures or rituals.
- Nazrul is a fair contrast, but for a different reason: he deliberately wrote across traditions (hamd/naat *and* Shyama Sangeet) as part of his own personal commitment to Hindu-Muslim unity. That was his individual project, not an obligation every writer owes.

As for the actual question: Rabindranath didn't really write "Hindu songs" either, in the sectarian sense. His devotional songs (the Puja porjay) address a formless, universal divine — shaped by his Brahmo Samaj upbringing, which rejected idol worship and ritualism. You'll find almost no songs invoking Kali, Krishna, or specific deities, just as there are none about Ma Amina. His spiritual idiom simply wasn't organized around any organized religion's iconography.

That said, Islamic and Persian influences run deep in his work. He grew up hearing his father Debendranath recite Hafiz of Shiraz, drew heavily on Baul and Sufi traditions (he championed Lalon Fakir's songs), and was honored in Iran as a spiritual heir of Hafiz. Nazrul's project was different — he consciously wrote both Islamic ghazals and Shyama Sangeet as a statement of Hindu-Muslim unity. Tagore's universalism took the opposite route: instead of writing for every religion, he wrote for none in particular. Ironically, that's exactly what made his songs feel "un-Islamic" to the Pakistani establishment — and impossible to ban from Bengali hearts."
